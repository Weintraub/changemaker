Problem Landscape

History

Before the first salmon canneries were built on the BC coast in the 1870s, Indigenous communities had already built flourishing economies based on salmon. Traditional fishing practices were disrupted by the introduction of settlers, and canning technology, to major salmon-bearing rivers. In 1852, Indigenous peoples signed the 1852 Douglas Treaty, which formally guaranteed their fisheries. Yet, in the 1890s, licenses were introduced that restricted the ability of Indigenous fishers to catch fish independently from canneries. Since then, little has been done to improve the situation arising.

Size

Though the fish farming industry in British Columbia has doubled its output between 1995 and 2005, the wild fishing industry decreased its output by 30% during that same time. A number of stakeholders have raised concerns about the environmental, social, and economic impacts of these farms.

Scope

Fish farms present a variety of problems for local salmon populations. Indeed, the majority of fish farms fish Atlantic salmon, which affects local Pacific salmon populations. Further, because of the density of fish in these pens, fish farming companies use drugs and pesticides to prevent disease -- fish that escape from these pens can spread disease to wild fish and have negative genetic impacts.
